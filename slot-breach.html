Slot Breach Response — Before & After the Framework

Same problem. Same data. Two very different responses. One was designed before the Customer Communication Framework existed. One is what the framework prescribes.

“Technician didn’t arrive on the confirmed date.”
~29% of all self-initiated cancellations happen after this exact moment.
Before the framework — what was designed

Three timed notifications, all identical

The original design fires the same channel stack (push + WhatsApp) at fixed intervals after the breach: T0, T+24 hours, and T+48 hours. Same tier. Same format. Same message pattern. The LCO/partner side is notified in parallel.

Same stack, 48 hours later
The framework’s problem with this: it fires on a clock, regardless of what’s happening. If a new slot is confirmed at T+6 hours, the T+24 and T+48 messages still go out — to an already-recovered customer. Same stack every time means no escalation when things get worse. And there’s no point at which a human picks up the phone.
After the framework — what we should ship

Event-driven, escalating by breach number

The framework fires on state changes, not on the clock. Each missed slot is a separate event — with escalating severity, different copy, and a clear stopping point. Internally, Wiom may retry with a new partner up to three times. From the customer’s side, each retry is a new promise that can be kept or broken.

The four structural shifts

Why the difference matters, in four moves

01
Clocks don’t matter. Events do.
The old design fires at 0h/24h/48h. The framework fires when something changes. A customer whose booking has recovered at hour 6 shouldn’t still be getting “please wait” pings at hour 24.
02
Different failures deserve different responses.
Breach #1 is a missed date. Breach #3 is a relationship failure. They cannot be the same message or the same channel stack. Severity escalates with every breach.
03
A human must show up before trust runs out.
After three broken promises, text cannot carry it. Tier 4 forces a CX call within two hours — with two concrete options, not another reassurance message.
04
The customer’s right to exit is a feature, not a bug.
At Breach #2, the framework proactively surfaces the cancel-with-refund option. Giving the customer a graceful way out is not losing them — it’s respecting that they’ve already been failed twice.
Why ship this version

What changes, measurably

The outcomes we can measure

  • Time-to-first-message drops from ~12 hours to 30 minutes. The current 48-hour batch is replaced with a 30-minute trigger.
  • Already-recovered customers stop getting pings. Exit logic suppresses the T+24 and T+48 fires once a new slot is confirmed.
  • Channel inflation is structurally prevented. SMS only fires if WA delivery fails. Human call only fires when text has been exhausted.
  • Breach #3 customers reach a human within 2 hours. Not a chatbot. Not another WhatsApp template. A person.
  • Refund option is offered — not buried. Customers who have been failed twice are given a dignified way out, in their own language, on the channel they’re already on.
Bottom line

The framework in one sentence

Wiom is pull-based. We leave the app only when the customer cannot find the truth by opening it — and when we do leave, we leave with discipline, escalation that matches the broken trust, and a human on the phone before the customer gives up.
